# Non-well-founded Hierarchies of Nested Systems

## Project Summary

This project investigates complex systems through recursive nesting of [[subsystem|subsystems]], examining emergence as a structural phenomenon in [[hierarchy|hierarchical]] architectures. Building on Simon's seminal observation that [[hierarchy]]—the architecture of complexity itself—emerges from the dynamics of nearly decomposable systems [[#Simon2012|(Simon, 1962)]], this investigation explores how [[subsystem|subsystems]] contain [[subsystem|subsystems]] in recursive patterns that may lead to non-well-founded structures.

The formal framework draws on Mesarovic and Takahara's mathematical foundations of general systems theory [[#Mesarovic1976|(Mesarovic & Takahara, 1975)]], which provides the set-theoretic substrate for defining [[system|systems]] as relations on Cartesian products. Their hierarchical systems theory [[#Mesarovic1970|(Mesarovic, Macko, & Takahara, 1970)]] establishes the theoretical foundation for understanding multilevel, [[nested-system|nested]] organizations where each level exhibits autonomous system properties while participating in larger wholes.

Backlund's analysis of system definitions [[#Backlund2000|(Backlund, 2000)]] informs our approach by emphasizing that system elements must not be isolated from other parts—a criterion crucial for understanding genuine systemic properties in [[nested-system|nested hierarchies]]. The central question is: How can we rigorously formalize the [[hierarchical-decomposition|hierarchical organization]] of complex systems where [[subsystem|subsystems]] themselves contain [[subsystem|subsystems]], potentially creating structures that challenge well-foundedness assumptions?

This investigation develops formal machinery for the enclose relation, subsystem dependencies, and emergent properties that arise when [[system|systems]] are organized in [[nested-system|nested hierarchies]].
